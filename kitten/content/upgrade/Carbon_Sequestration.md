[Carbon Sequestration]( {{< relref "upgrade/Carbon_Sequestration.md" >}}) is a late-game Workshop upgrade that allows your civilization to actively mitigate the negative effects of **pollution**. To unlock this upgrade, you must first research the [Ecology]( {{< relref "techs/Ecology.md" >}}) science technology.

### **Costs and Prerequisites**
*   **Science Cost:** 75,000.
*   **Resource Costs:** 1,250 Titanium, 125 Gears, 4,000 Steel, and 1,000 Alloy.
*   **Prerequisite Technology:** [Ecology]( {{< relref "techs/Ecology.md" >}}) (which itself requires Combustion and 55 Blueprints).

### **Core Effects and Mechanics**
The upgrade transforms your **Factories** from industrial polluters into tools for environmental recovery:
*   **Factory Pollution Reduction:** It immediately reduces the pollution produced by all Factories to **zero**.
*   **Active Carbon Capture:** It enables a toggle for Factories to capture pollution from the atmosphere. When this mode is activated, each Factory consumes **4 Watts** of energy (double its standard requirement) and reduces the global pollution level by **2 points per tick**.
*   **Enhanced UI and Management:** The upgrade provides **more detailed information** via the pollution indicator in the Science tab and adds **+/- buttons** to all polluting buildings. This allows you to manually enable or disable specific structures to balance resource production against environmental health.

### **Strategic Utility**
Managing pollution is critical because high levels of atmospheric CO2 negatively impact several areas of your village:
*   **Production Penalties:** High pollution reduces **catnip production**, which can lead to kitten starvation.
*   **Civilian Penalties:** It decreases **kitten happiness** and slows down the **arrival speed** of new kittens.
*   **Religious Penalties:** Eventually, extreme pollution can reduce the **Solar Revolution bonus** for certain resources.

While some pollution is considered inevitable, Carbon Sequestration is one of the few active methods to keep levels below the **~1,000 ppm threshold**, where negative effects are generally negligible.
